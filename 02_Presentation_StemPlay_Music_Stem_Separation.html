Audio analysis

~

Envelope

Smoothed amplitude follower.
Snaps to hits, decays naturally.

Ξ

Average Energy

Mean of all FFT frequency bins,
normalized to [0, 1].

◊

Beat Signal

Fires when energy crosses
a threshold, with cooldown.

"Each stem gets its own analyser running a 2048-point FFT. From that we extract three things: First, an envelope follower. It has fast attack so visuals react instantly to hits, but slow release so they do not flicker. This is the single most important parameter. Get it wrong and the whole thing feels either sluggish or jittery. Second, average energy. Just the mean of all frequency bins. This drives the overall intensity of each sculpture. Third, a beat signal. Specifically for drums. When the energy crosses a threshold, it fires, but with a cooldown so it does not double-trigger. That drives the particle bursts on the columns."

The secret ingredient: reactive lighting

"The sculptures are the stars, but the lighting is what ties the room together. Every spotlight in this museum is subtly reacting to the audio. Drum hits brighten the warm point light. Vocal energy lifts the top spotlight. Bass shifts the side fills. You do not consciously notice it when it is there. But take it away and the scene feels dead. It is like film lighting, you only notice bad lighting, never good lighting. The camera also responds. Drum hits cause a subtle shake. Bass pulls it closer. Combined with mouse parallax and idle drift, the whole room breathes."

Stem-to-sculpture mapping

Stem Sculpture Reactive behavior
Drums Lattice Columns (x4) Vertical stretch on beat, emissive glow, dust particle bursts
Bass Chest Statue Morph-target inflation, glowing core sphere, positional lift
Vocals Fracture Bust Animation timeline scrubbing driven by vocal envelope
Other Chandelier + Painting Pendulum swing, spin, painting fragment drift and sway
"Each mapping was designed to match the character of the sound. Drums are percussive and instantaneous, so the columns snap vertically and emit burst particles. It is the most physically violent response. Bass is about weight and sustain, so the statue inflates using morph targets. A red core sphere pulses inside on transients. Vocals are expressive and continuous, so we tied them to a fracture animation. The louder the vocal energy, the more the bust cracks open. It scrubs through the animation timeline. And everything else, synths, pads, atmospherics, drives the chandelier which swings like a pendulum, and the painting whose fragments drift apart and sway."

What we learned

1
Envelope smoothing makes or breaks the feel.
Fast attack, slow release. Get the ratio wrong and visuals feel either sluggish or jittery.
2
Reactive lighting is the glue.
The sculptures alone look like separate animations. Audio-driven lighting ties the whole room into one cohesive experience.
3
The web platform is ready for this.
Web Audio API, WebGL, and React's component model handle real-time audio-visual work surprisingly well.
Go through each one briefly: "Three big takeaways. First, the envelope smoothing parameters matter more than anything else. We spent a lot of time tuning the attack and release values. Too fast and everything flickers. Too slow and it feels disconnected from the music. Second, audio-reactive lighting is what makes the scene feel like one thing instead of four separate animations. It is subtle, but it is the difference between a tech demo and something that feels alive. Third, we were surprised by how capable the web platform is for this kind of real-time work. Web Audio API is solid, WebGL handles the rendering, and React Three Fiber makes it manageable."
